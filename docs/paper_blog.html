Hidden activations know when self-report does not

A claim-level uncertainty study across MAUD legal QA and FActScore biographies: Llama says it is confident, but a linear probe on its residual stream sees more.

STEPHEN CASELLA · APRIL 2026

Paper draft References FActScore CIs MAUD CIs
TL;DR

Llama 3.1 8B's structured confidence does not reliably reveal which claims are unreliable. On MAUD merger-agreement QA, self-report reaches 0.511 AUROC; a linear probe on layer-19 residual activations reaches 0.771, a paired delta of 0.260 [0.137, 0.387]. On FActScore biographies, using human atomic-fact labels, self-report reaches 0.541; the residual probe reaches 0.802, delta 0.262 [0.219, 0.302].

The result is not "the model knows truth." It is more specific and more useful: hidden activations contain recoverable claim-level correctness signal that the model's own verbal confidence does not faithfully expose. The boundary is just as important: FELM is directionally positive but inconclusive, and its matched-generation repair shows how reference-bounded labels can measure evidence coverage rather than correctness.

The question

Long answers fail locally. One sentence may be right, the next partly right, and the next confidently wrong. If the model could reliably say which claims were shaky, a simple self-report baseline would be enough.

In this study, it is not enough. We show Llama 3.1 8B fixed claims and ask for structured confidence. Then we compare that confidence to a linear probe trained on the model's own hidden activations. The probe is deliberately simple: layer-19 residual vectors, pooled over the claim span, with logistic regression on top.

The claim

Across two usable domains, residual activations recover claim-level correctness signal that structured self-report misses. The effect size is almost identical on MAUD and FActScore, even though the protocols and labels differ.

Setup — three datasets, one probed model

The probed model is always Llama 3.1 8B Instruct. What changes is the dataset and label source. MAUD gives us fixed legal claims scored by two LLM judges. FActScore gives us human-labeled biography atomic facts. FELM gives us human segment labels, but its evidence structure turns out to be less clean for the claim-level target.

01 · MAUD Legal QA, judge-proxy labels 150 frozen claim units from merger-agreement question answering. GPT-5.4 labels define the primary correctness target; Kimi K2.6 provides a second-judge sensitivity pass.
02 · FActScore Biographies, human labels 4,886 non-IR human atomic facts from ChatGPT biographies. Atomic facts rarely appear verbatim, so activations are pooled over parent sentence spans.
03 · FELM-wk Boundary case World-knowledge segments show the same direction but not significance. A matched-generation repair produces too many not-enough-evidence labels to scale cleanly.
04 · Baseline Structured self-report Llama is shown the fixed claim unit and asked for confidence. This is an easy version of self-report: failures cannot be blamed on implicit claim boundaries.

The headline — probes beat self-report

The main comparison is simple: how well does each score rank correct claims above not-correct claims? AUROC is 0.5 at chance. Higher is better.

MAUD — the legal QA core

MAUD is the first setting: merger-agreement question answering with 150 fixed claims. GPT-5.4 labels provide the first judge-proxy target, and Kimi K2.6 provides an independent family sensitivity check.

Under the GPT-5.4 judge, the residual probe reaches 0.771 AUROC and Llama self-report reaches 0.511. A GPT-5.4 external scorer is much stronger, at 0.944, but that number is inflated because GPT-5.4 also produced the labels. Under the Kimi judge, the same scorer drops to 0.872. That is still the best method, but 0.87 is the fairer estimate.

The agreement set matters

On the 110 claims where GPT-5.4 and Kimi agree, every strong method gets cleaner. GPT-5.4 scoring reaches 0.981 AUROC, the residual probe reaches 0.793, and the SAE probe reaches 0.712. That pattern is evidence against a pure judge-artifact story. If a probe were mainly fitting GPT-5.4's quirks, it should not improve when restricted to labels another judge family also accepts.

FActScore — the transfer check

FActScore changes almost everything: human labels instead of LLM judges, biographies instead of contracts, ChatGPT generations instead of Llama generations, and atomic facts rather than extracted legal claims. The protocol is also different because the dataset is much larger: FActScore supports a clean 70/15/15 split by biography, while MAUD's 150 claims use leave-one-out evaluation.

Table 03FActScore held-out test results
Method Test claims AUROC Brier
Llama self-report 742 0.541 [0.514, 0.570] 0.298 [0.262, 0.330]
Residual probe 742 0.802 [0.769, 0.833] 0.171 [0.156, 0.189]

The probe diagnostics are healthy: train AUROC 0.939, validation 0.728, test 0.802. Test AUROC above validation is not surprising given biography-level splits, where individual biographies vary substantially in difficulty and fact density. The pattern is consistent with the C=0.01 model generalizing across that variation rather than overfitting to validation.

Why this is a replication, not a copy

MAUD and FActScore do not share the same protocol. What repeats is the qualitative gap: self-report is near chance; residual activations recover correctness signal; the paired AUROC delta is about +0.26 in both settings.

The boundary — why FELM is weaker

FELM-wk points in the same direction: residual probe 0.652, self-report 0.511. But the delta interval crosses zero, so it is not a clean positive result. More importantly, the matched-generation repair exposed an annotation-target problem.

When Llama generated new FELM-style answers and those segments were judged against fuller reference-bounded evidence, 30 of 52 segments became not_enough_evidence. That is 57.7 percent of the pilot. The result is not "FELM fails"; it is that reference-bounded labels can become coverage measurements when generated claims exceed the reference scope.

The wide FELM interval reflects both the smaller test set and noisier label structure. A more definitive FELM result would require either substantially more data or annotation methodology that directly addresses the reference-coverage problem.

Honest scope
  • Does show: residual activations recover correctness signal on MAUD and FActScore, while structured self-report does not.
  • Does not show: probes detect legal truth or factual truth in an absolute sense. MAUD remains judge-proxy evidence until the human audit lands.
  • Does show: GPT-5.4 external scoring is strongest on MAUD, but its 0.944 same-family number is inflated; 0.872 under Kimi is the fairer estimate.
  • Does not show: residual probes reliably beat SAE probes. Residual is directionally stronger, but the residual-vs-SAE delta is not statistically stable across judges and agreement-set scoring.

The methodological lesson

The strongest lesson is not "legal QA transfers to biography QA." It is that label provenance dominates. MAUD works because fixed claims are judged by a clear rubric. FActScore works because human atomic-fact labels directly capture factual support. FELM is weaker because segment labels and reference coverage are coarser than the claim-level correctness target we want. Claim construction and label provenance affect probe trainability as much as model architecture choices do.

For a probe-based uncertainty system, the hard questions come before training the classifier. What is the claim? What does the label mean? Does the activation span match the label unit? If those three line up, a simple residual probe can recover signal. If they do not, a better probe will mostly learn a muddier target.

· · ·

The result is an interpretability win in the practical sense: a linear readout of hidden activations sees something the model's own confidence report misses. The win is bounded, not mystical. It does not give us a compact SAE feature story, a legal ground truth adjudicator, or a domain-general hallucination detector. Downstream tools building on this result should expose label-provenance and claim-construction assumptions rather than hiding them behind opaque confidence scores.
