Llama's activations know more than its confidence An interpretability-first judge-proxy study showing that hidden states recover claim-level correctness signal in merger-agreement question answering.

SC Sam Casella · ~12 min read · merger agreements · 135 examples · 150 claims
TL;DR What was tested: I broke legal answers into small factual claims and asked which signal best predicts claim correctness: Llama's own confidence, a GPT-5.4 external scorer, or probes trained on hidden activations.

What happened: The interpretability result is the win: raw Llama activations carried meaningful claim-correctness signal across two judge families, while Llama's own self-reported confidence stayed near random.

What not to overclaim: GPT-5.4 also supplies the proxy labels, so the GPT-5.4 external scorer is same-family consistency, not independent legal truth. The activation-probe result is evidence of internal correctness signal, not proof of absolute legal correctness.
Reader contract All reported correctness is proxy correctness on MAUD merger-agreement claims, first against GPT-5.4 labels and then stress-tested with Kimi K2.6. These are not independent legal-truth labels, and the result should be read as an interpretability methods result with proxy-label boundaries.
Reader map If you care about interpretability, start with the headline result, probe, and agreement-set sections. If you care about legal-data validity, read the CUAD boundary and limits sections. If you care about reproducibility, the artifact links are at the bottom.

The useful surprise in this run is an interpretability win: Llama's hidden activations carried a real signal about which legal claims were correct, even when the model's stated confidence barely ranked wrong claims better than chance. In this setting, the model's internals knew more than its self-report.

Here the domain is legal question answering over merger agreements. MAUD is the merger-agreement benchmark used in the final run. A "claim" is a small factual statement extracted from a model answer, such as whether a contract has a particular termination fee or approval condition. Scoring claims instead of whole answers lets every method compete on the same units of meaning.

The experimental design was deliberately strict. The claim list is frozen before scoring. Self-report, GPT-5.4 decomposition, and activation probes all score the same claims. A judge LLM supplies a scalable proxy label for correctness, whether the claim matters to the final answer, and the evidence that would change the answer if the claim flipped. The goal is not to settle legal truth; it is to ask whether internal representations expose correctness information that the model does not surface verbally.

The live question When the entire pipeline is run on the MAUD merger-agreement benchmark with judge-proxy labels, do activation probes reveal claim-level correctness signal that self-report hides?

That question is useful because it makes the comparison operational. It tells us whether the pipeline works end to end, whether the task family is viable, whether self-report is even competitive, and whether the model's internals contain enough signal to justify deeper validation. The broader CUAD transfer attempt did not pass its readiness screen, which becomes a boundary result later in the post.

The question we set out to answer

The protocol compares four scoring methods:

A1 · self-report Llama rates its own answer claims Llama 3.1 8B Instruct emits structured confidence scores for the frozen claim list. This is the direct "ask the model how sure it is" baseline.
A2 · external decomposition GPT-5.4 external scorer, same judge family GPT-5.4 scores the same frozen claims from the outside. It is powerful, expensive, and useful as a high-end comparison point, but it is not independent from the GPT-5.4 judge-proxy labels.
B1 · residual probe Raw activation features A small classifier reads hidden-state vectors from the answer tokens. This tests whether the model's internal activations contain correctness signal.
B2 · SAE probe Goodfire SAE features Sparse autoencoder, or SAE, features are a more compressed and interpretable view of the same answer-token activations. This tests whether that basis improves over raw residuals.

The starting dataset contract was broader than the final analysis: 150 MAUD merger-agreement question-answering examples and 150 CUAD-derived examples from other kinds of contracts. CUAD, the Contract Understanding Atticus Dataset, is a broader contract-review benchmark. The intent was to test whether the same claim-level protocol would transfer across legal contract families. That broader design taught us something important: not every legal question-answering source produces stable claim objects under the same generation setup.

Why the labels are proxy labels

The judge-proxy protocol is the center of the study, not an administrative shortcut. It gives us a fixed, reproducible label source over the same frozen claims scored by every method. In a claim-level uncertainty experiment, that shared label surface matters more than labeling ceremony: every method must be evaluated on the same unit of meaning.

CUAD did not pass readiness for a task-specific reason: it did not behave like the same claim-level generation task. Even after prompt cleanup, the CUAD side produced too many off-target answers and unstable claim fragments. The conservative decision was to keep CUAD as a documented boundary case and run the judge-proxy comparison on MAUD, where the claim surface is coherent.

What the labels mean judge_llm_proxy labels are the experimental reference labels for this study. They are not legal adjudications and should not be read as legal truth. The claim is about method ranking under a reproducible judge protocol.

What the study actually measures

The final MAUD judge-proxy run exercises the intended pipeline end to end. We have fixed answer runs, frozen claims, judge labels, self-report predictions, GPT-5.4 external predictions, raw activation features, SAE features, resampling-entropy targets, and paraphrase-stability targets. In plain terms: we measure whether an answer stays stable when the model is sampled again, and whether it stays stable when the question is rephrased.

Correctness is the main signal. The judge labels have useful class variety: 62 true, 68 partially_true, and 20 false. The primary correctness score is strict: partially_true is counted as incorrect.

Load-bearingness, meaning whether a claim actually matters to the final answer, is less healthy as a target. The proxy labels are 138 yes and 12 no. That makes load-bearing metrics possible in the baseline table, but not something I would build a strong claim on. The headline results below use the stricter proxy-correctness target.

The headline result: hidden-state signal survives

Two metrics carry most of the result. AUROC is a ranking score: 0.5 is random, 1.0 is perfect. Brier score measures probability error: lower is better.

The interpretability result is that simple linear probes on Llama activations recover correctness signal that the model's own confidence does not expose. On the full MAUD judge-proxy set, the residual probe reaches AUROC 0.7709, while Llama self-report lands at 0.5114, barely above random ranking. That is the main win: internal states separate correct from not-true legal claims better than the model's verbal confidence.

GPT-5.4 external scoring is still the strongest comparator: AUROC 0.9435, Brier 0.1610. But it is not the interpretability result, and it is not free evidence that the task is solved: the first judge is also GPT-5.4. The result is best read as a ceiling-style external scorer plus an activation-probe finding below it, not as an independent legal-truth baseline.

Second-judge correction The later Kimi K2.6 pass keeps the ordering intact but lowers the defensible GPT-5.4 scorer estimate from 0.944 to 0.872. The activation-probe result also survives the judge-family change: residual activations score 0.707 under Kimi and 0.793 on the agreement set, while self-report remains near random.

What the probes say

A probe is a small model trained on internal features from a larger model. Here it asks the interpretability question directly: do hidden activations around an answer contain information about whether a claim is correct?

The probe story has two layers. The quick check asks whether the feature files contain correctness signal at all by repeatedly holding out one example and training on the rest. There the answer is yes: raw residual activations and SAE features both carry signal. Residuals are directionally stronger in the full-corpus quick check, but the bootstrap evidence supports the narrower claim that the two probe representations are comparable.

The richer train/validation/test pass adds the planned target structure. One probe predicts resampling entropy, a measure of how unstable the answer is across repeated generations. A second, simpler probe predicts correctness directly. The split is small: 105 train claims, 23 validation claims, and 22 test claims. So the numbers are useful for direction, not for a final claim.

This is why I would not yet claim "SAE is worse" or "SAE is better." In the feature-only quick check, residual is directionally ahead, but the second-judge and agreement-set bootstrap intervals make the gap inconclusive. In the small train/test split, the SAE entropy probe edges out the residual entropy probe. The robust statement is more precise: SAE has not clearly improved the story over raw residuals. It is interesting enough to keep, not strong enough to headline.

The probe path fix mattered The activation path now reads the exact token spans from the original answer run. That was not cosmetic. If feature extraction regenerates answers later, or splits them into tokens differently, it can silently attach features to the wrong text. This study is valuable partly because it forced that bug into the open.

The CUAD boundary result

Negative results are easy to treat as administrative detail. This one is substantive. The CUAD prompt-and-extraction setup did not pass a 15-example readiness screen because it did not produce stable claim-level objects under the same generation setup. In other words, the answers did not reliably decompose into clean factual claims. That means "legal question answering" was too broad a category for one shared claim protocol.

Why CUAD stayed out of scope
  • CUAD answers were often off-target. Nine of fifteen CUAD screening examples had suspect answers.
  • Claims became unstable. Seven CUAD examples produced unusable claims, with thirteen unusable claims in the initial mixed sample.
  • The shared-claim premise broke. If the frozen claim list is unstable, no downstream comparison is fair.
  • MAUD remained viable. The MAUD screening check passed, which is why MAUD became the analysis scope.

The practical lesson is that MAUD-style merger-agreement question answering is a viable substrate for this claim-level uncertainty setup. CUAD probably needs a different upstream extraction pipeline: tighter evidence windows, task-specific answer formats, or a non-generative field-extraction framing.

What this result does and does not show

This result has useful signal, but its boundaries are sharp.

What this is not This is not a claim about legal truth. It is not the mixed MAUD+CUAD study. It is also not a clean proof that GPT-5.4 is "right"; GPT-5.4 is both the judge labeler and the strongest external baseline, so that comparison is partly self-consistency.

The strongest safe claim is methodological and directional. On this MAUD judge-proxy run, hidden activations carry useful proxy-correctness signal that Llama self-report does not expose, and SAE features are not clearly superior to residual features. The overall pipeline is complete enough to support a proxy-label interpretability result.

Threats to validity The main limits are no human legal label surface yet, moderate judge disagreement on borderline claims, a small held-out probe test set, possible non-independence among claims from nearby source examples, and the fact that CUAD was only screened under this prompt-and-extraction setup.

Second-judge update

I reran the frozen 150-claim label pass with a second judge from a different model family, moonshotai/kimi-k2.6 on Prime Intellect. The story moved in magnitude but not in ordering: against Kimi labels, Llama self-report was 0.466 [0.413, 0.604] AUROC, GPT-5.4 external scoring was 0.872 [0.807, 0.926], the residual probe was 0.707 [0.627, 0.792], and the SAE probe was 0.652 [0.563, 0.735]. The two judges agreed on 73.3% of claims, with Cohen's kappa 0.572 [0.456, 0.682]. On the 110-claim agreement set, the same ordering held: GPT-5.4 external scoring 0.981 [0.931, 1.000], residual probe 0.793 [0.709, 0.870], SAE probe 0.712 [0.616, 0.812], and Llama self-report 0.486 [0.392, 0.598]. Intervals are 95% paired bootstrap CIs over claims. That makes the interpretability headline more robust than the first-pass result alone, while still keeping the claim in proxy-label territory rather than legal-truth territory.

What the agreement set tells us

The agreement set is the most useful slice, not just a robustness appendix. All strong methods improve when the two judge families agree: GPT-5.4 reaches 0.981, the residual probe reaches 0.793, and the SAE probe reaches 0.712. Llama self-report stays at noise. That pattern suggests the probes are tracking a shared claim-correctness signal rather than only one judge's quirks. It also says the next ceiling is label quality: once labels are less noisy, the internal-state methods separate claims more cleanly.

The paired bootstrap deltas make the SAE conclusion narrower. Residual beats self-report under both judges and on the agreement set. GPT-5.4 beats residual. Residual is directionally ahead of SAE, but residual-vs-SAE does not reliably exclude zero under Kimi or on the agreement set. The honest claim is that SAE does not clearly improve over raw residuals here.

One calibration wrinkle is worth noting but not headlining: Llama self-report's Brier score improves under Kimi even though its AUROC remains noise. That suggests the confidence values happen to be better calibrated to Kimi's label distribution than GPT-5.4's; it is not a ranking improvement.

What would strengthen the claim

The study is now coherent as a MAUD-focused judge-proxy result under two LLM label surfaces. The next work is to connect that proxy stability to a small human legal audit.

Judge robustness

The second-judge check is now complete. Kimi K2.6 changed the absolute AUROCs but preserved the method ordering, and the agreement-set result sharpened the same story. The remaining robustness question is not another LLM judge; it is whether a small source-bound human audit agrees with the proxy-label structure.

Report the CUAD boundary as a result

CUAD should not be hidden as setup trouble. It is evidence that the claim-level protocol needs a stable generative question-answering surface. MAUD has it; CUAD, as currently prompted, does not. That boundary makes the methods claim sharper.

· · ·

The project has crossed an important threshold. It is no longer a pile of setup scripts and partial artifacts. It is a complete first-pass judge-proxy experiment with a clear empirical shape plus a second-judge sensitivity check. Llama self-report did not rank proxy correctness on this MAUD setup. Raw activations recovered a meaningful part of that signal. SAE features perform comparably rather than decisively better or worse. A powerful external judge remains the strongest comparator, even after switching the label family, but the interpretability win is that Llama's hidden states know something its self-report does not.

The result is not the last word on claim truth. It is a clean proxy-label answer to the question this study was built to ask: under a reproducible judge, which uncertainty signals actually rank the claims? The next proof point is a source-bound human audit sample.
